Beyond the Lemon Grove

The Geographic Eviction of the American Film Industry

First draft complete. Currently seeking representation.

Why this coordinate and not another.
Primary sources cited per chapter.
Eleven verified original discoveries.
The sign is not a dream. It is a boundary.
The floor is not a crop. The floor is a function. Hollywood was not discovered, chosen, or predestined. It was occupied — in the 1,367‑day gap between a water deal and a water delivery — by an industry that moved into a bankrupt temperance town’s devalued roadhouse for thirty dollars a month and never left. Film didn’t create the floor. The floor selected film — once — and never let go.

Beyond the Lemon Grove is narrative nonfiction built entirely from primary documents — a forensic geographic and economic audit that places the reader on the ground in 1894 when the surveyor measures the floor, in 1911 when the industry occupies it, in 1923 when the sign rises above it, and in 1949 when four letters are removed and the mechanism disappears.

The standard explanation for Hollywood’s emergence — sunshine, cheap land, flight from the Edison Trust — explains why the industry moved west. It does not explain why it landed on a specific coordinate and stayed. The decisive factor was terrain. Early one‑reel chase films thrived in the canyons of Edendale. Feature‑length filmmaking could not. The flat Hollywood floor made a new cinematic grammar possible, and once capital recognized that fact it escalated rapidly and locked in place. The floor always becomes whatever the ridge doesn’t want — but can’t function without. This book names the mechanism for the first time.

The manuscript documents eleven verified discoveries derived from primary sources — including a quantifiable daily production penalty derived from an 1894 federal survey, a 1,367‑day sovereignty gap that left the floor legally undefended, and the forensic proof that the Hollywoodland sign was erected above an already‑locked industrial basin, not above a dream.

Author

Kenny Abramowitz holds an MFA in Screenwriting from the AFI Conservatory and an MLIS, with four years of professional experience as a California public librarian specializing in collection development.

His undergraduate training in motion picture technology included early and silent film history under Charles Harpole, editor of the History of the American Cinema series.
