Beyond the Lemon Grove: The Geographic Eviction of the American Film Industry is narrative nonfiction, approximately 90,000 words. The book traces a documented causal chain from an 1894 federal survey — measuring the Hollywood‑Colegrove corridor as a flat, zero‑percent‑grade alluvial plain at 356 feet above sea level — to the 1949 contract that removed four letters from a sign above the basin, transforming a territorial boundary marker into a global abstract icon.

The standard explanation for Hollywood’s emergence — sunshine, cheap land, flight from the Edison Trust — explains why the industry moved west. It does not explain why it landed on a specific coordinate and stayed. The decisive factor was terrain. Early one‑reel chase films thrived in the canyons of Edendale. Feature‑length filmmaking could not. The flat Hollywood floor made a new cinematic grammar possible, and once capital recognized that fact it escalated rapidly and locked in place. The floor always becomes whatever the ridge doesn’t want — but can’t function without. This book names the mechanism for the first time.

The manuscript documents eleven verified discoveries derived from primary sources — including a quantifiable daily production penalty derived from an 1894 federal survey, a 1,367‑day sovereignty gap that left the floor legally undefended, and the forensic proof that the Hollywoodland sign was erected above an already‑locked industrial basin, not above a dream.
